%% Abstract
\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

AI-assisted healthcare screening systems have demonstrated strong predictive performance but face persistent adoption barriers for the families expected to use them: caregivers need understandable, private, affordable, and clinician-governed support before they will trust home-based ASD screening. This dissertation investigates a B2C-primary, B2B-supported pathway for AI-assisted Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) electroencephalography (EEG) screening, in which caregivers and patients are the primary value beneficiaries and clinicians, hospitals, and governance controls provide validation, escalation, and safety oversight.

The locked four-box conceptual model --- \textbf{Governance $\to$ Explainability $\to$ Trust $\to$ Adoption} --- is operationalised through six hypotheses (H1--H6) and tested through clinician-side structural evidence ($n = 131$) using Partial Least Squares Structural Equation Modelling (PLS-SEM) with 1{,}000-resample bootstrap mediation analysis, complemented by caregiver-facing trust, usability, and ecosystem-governance design evidence. The hero managerial artefact is the \textbf{Responsible Generative AI Governance (RGAIG) Maturity Framework}: a six-level maturity model (Initial $\to$ Documented $\to$ Measured $\to$ Controlled $\to$ Adaptive $\to$ Operationalised) that supports a consumer-facing ASD screening pathway while keeping clinical review, auditability, and escalation under provider governance.

Theoretical anchoring extends the Technology Acceptance Model (Davis, 1989) by positioning Responsible AI governance as a structural antecedent of perceived explainability, with downstream effects on calibrated trust (Lee \& See, 2004) and adoption readiness within socio-technical systems (Cherns, 1976). The technical operationalisation --- ensemble classifiers reaching 97.67\% accuracy on the ASD-EEG empirical task, SHAP-based attribution, and Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) for citation-grounded explanation --- is treated as supporting infrastructure for the governance-trust-adoption pathway, not as the dissertation's primary contribution.

Expert-rated explainability ($M = 4.6/5$, $\alpha = 0.84$) and a structural mediation analysis confirmed that governance maturity strengthens clinician trust through perceived explainability, and that trust mediates adoption readiness. Findings indicate that the B2C value proposition is strongest when the device is positioned as caregiver-facing early support and continuous monitoring, not autonomous diagnosis: the family receives plain-language explanations, trend reports, and escalation guidance, while B2B partners provide clinician confirmation, hospital SOS response, EHR integration, auditability, and regulatory governance. The dissertation contributes (i)~a B2C-primary adoption logic for AI-assisted ASD-EEG screening, (ii)~an empirically tested governance-explainability-trust-adoption pathway that makes B2B governance the safety layer for consumer use, (iii)~the RGAIG Maturity Framework as the practical artefact for clinically governed deployment, (iv)~a theoretical extension of TAM with governance as structural antecedent, and (v)~a defensibility scaffold (13 Defensibility Packs across the chapters and appendices) that supports doctoral examination. Scope remains bounded: B2C public launch requires phased validation of consumer-grade wearable accuracy, affordability, privacy, comfort, and caregiver trust before full-scale deployment.

\tablestripes
\tablefontsize

\toprule
\thead{Element} & \thead{Standard} \\
\midrule

\addlinespace
Header font & Bold, ggunavy colour, via \texttt{\textbackslash thead\{\}} command \\
\addlinespace
Column padding & 4\,pt (\texttt{\textbackslash tabcolsep}) — consistent across all tables \\
\addlinespace
Table width & Full text width (\texttt{\textbackslash textwidth}) using \texttt{tabularx} or \texttt{xltabular} \\
\addlinespace
Row separators & Booktabs only (\texttt{\textbackslash toprule}, \texttt{\textbackslash midrule}, \texttt{\textbackslash bottomrule}) — no vertical lines \\
\addlinespace
Row striping & Alternating grey rows via \texttt{\textbackslash tablestripes} \\
\addlinespace
Caption position & Above the table, numbered per chapter \\
\addlinespace
Note position & Below the table in italics, explaining abbreviations and data sources \\
\addlinespace
Column alignment & Left-aligned text columns; centred numeric columns \\
\addlinespace
Word breaking & Enabled — LaTeX may hyphenate words to prevent text overflow \\

\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Scholarly Foundation Summary}
\label{sec:scholarly_foundation_pack}

\noindent\textbf{What this section proves.} The bibliography that follows is interdisciplinary, theoretically grounded, and balanced across five citation clusters: theoretical foundations, governance and trust scholarship, healthcare AI literature, methodology authorities, and contemporary AI engineering. It is \emph{not} an AI-tooling collection assembled from recent papers. This pack documents the target balance, names the foundational authors in each cluster, and identifies the contradictory-literature anchors that prevent confirmation bias.

\section*{What Is Deliberately De-emphasized in the Bibliography}
\label{sec:scholarly_deemphasized}

\noindent The following citation categories are intentionally minimized in the bibliography to maintain scholarly balance:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Vendor / framework documentation.} References to LangChain docs, OpenAI API docs, Databricks documentation, etc.\ are used as supporting technical context only --- not as central academic anchors. Where the framework matters scientifically, the underlying peer-reviewed paper is cited instead (e.g., Lewis et al.~\citep{lewis2020rag} rather than the LangChain RAG documentation).
    \item \textbf{Conference-only AI papers.} While some recent conference papers are cited (e.g., for current SHAP / RAG methodology), the bibliography prioritises journal articles for theoretical claims --- conferences alone are weaker academic anchors.
    \item \textbf{Hype-cycle 2023--2025 AI papers.} The bibliography is bounded in time: foundational works (1951--2010) for theory; mid-period (2011--2020) for methodology and adoption studies; contemporary (2020--2025) for RAG, XAI operationalization, and EU AI Act regulatory context. Recent papers are cited where necessary, not for novelty.
    \item \textbf{Speculative AI futures.} References to AGI, quantum AI, swarm intelligence, neuromorphic AI, autonomous-civilization scenarios are explicitly absent from the bibliography. The dissertation's scope discipline (Section~\ref{sec:scope_defensibility_pack} in Appendix~H) excludes these from the contribution; the bibliography reflects that exclusion.
\end{itemize}

\noindent\textbf{End of Scholarly Foundation Summary.} The references that follow are formatted per the IEEE numbered style throughout, with DOIs included where available. Citation balance and theoretical grounding are the primary criteria for inclusion; AI-engineering currency is secondary.
